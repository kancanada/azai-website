How to Evaluate an ETF: 5 Factors That Matter

Not all ETFs are created equal. Here are the five factors that separate great ETFs from mediocre ones.

Beyond Just Returns

Many investors pick ETFs based solely on past returns. But a great ETF needs to score well across multiple factors. Here are the five that matter most.

1. Cost Efficiency (Expense Ratio)

The expense ratio is the annual fee you pay, expressed as a percentage. A 0.03% expense ratio means you pay $3/year per $10,000 invested. A 0.75% ratio means $75.

Over 30 years, the difference between 0.03% and 0.75% on a $100,000 investment is over $40,000. Costs compound against you just like returns compound for you.

2. Performance

Look at 3-year and 5-year returns, not just the past year. Short-term performance is noisy; longer periods reveal whether the ETF's strategy actually works.

3. Yield

If you need income, dividend yield matters. Some ETFs like JEPQ and JEPI are specifically designed for high yield. Others like QQQ prioritize growth with minimal dividends.

4. Size and Liquidity

Larger ETFs (measured by total assets) tend to have tighter bid-ask spreads, meaning you pay less when buying and selling. ETFs under $100 million in assets can have liquidity issues.

5. Diversification

Check the top 10 holdings concentration. If 40% of the ETF is in just 5 stocks, you're not as diversified as you think. True diversification spreads risk across many positions.

Key Takeaways

  • Low expense ratios compound into massive savings over decades
  • Judge performance over 3-5 years, not 3 months
  • Match yield expectations to your income needs
  • Prefer large, liquid ETFs with broad diversification
