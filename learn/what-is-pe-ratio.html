What Is a P/E Ratio? How to Use It Without Getting Fooled

The P/E ratio is the most popular valuation metric — and the most misused. Here is how to read it correctly and what it actually tells you about a stock.

Price Divided by Earnings

The Price-to-Earnings ratio tells you how much investors pay for each dollar of a company's earnings. A P/E of 20 means you're paying $20 for every $1 the company earns annually.

P/E Ratio = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share (EPS)

Example:

A stock trades at $150 and earns $7.50/share. The P/E ratio is 150 / 7.50 = 20x. You're paying 20 years' worth of current earnings for this stock.

What Different P/E Ranges Mean

  • Under 10: Often signals deep value or serious problems. Dig deeper before assuming it's a bargain.
  • 10-15: Generally considered undervalued. Common in mature, slow-growth industries.
  • 15-25: Fairly valued by historical standards. The S&P 500 average hovers around 20-22.
  • 25-40: Growth premium. Investors expect earnings to grow significantly.
  • 40+: Very high expectations baked in. Any earnings miss can cause sharp declines.

Common Mistakes

Comparing across industries

A tech company with a P/E of 30 isn't necessarily more expensive than a utility with a P/E of 15. Different industries have different growth rates and risk profiles. Always compare P/E within the same sector.

Ignoring earnings quality

A low P/E means nothing if earnings are about to decline. A company laying off workers and losing market share might have a P/E of 8, but that's a trap, not a bargain.

Using P/E alone

P/E is one data point, not a complete analysis. Combine it with intrinsic value calculations, EPS trends, and book value for a fuller picture.

Trailing vs Forward P/E

Trailing P/E uses the last 12 months of actual earnings. It's factual but backward-looking.

Forward P/E uses analyst estimates for next year. It's forward-looking but based on predictions that may be wrong.

Value investors generally prefer trailing P/E because it's based on real numbers, not guesses.

Key Takeaways

  • P/E tells you how much you pay per dollar of earnings
  • Compare P/E within industries, not across them
  • A low P/E is not automatically a bargain — check why it's low
  • Use P/E alongside other metrics, never in isolation
