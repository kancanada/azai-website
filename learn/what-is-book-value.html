What Is Book Value? Reading a Company's Balance Sheet

Book value tells you what a company would be worth if it closed its doors today and sold everything. Here is why it matters and when to use it.

The Liquidation Value

Book value is what remains if a company sells all its assets and pays off all its debts. It's the accounting net worth of the business, divided by shares outstanding.

Book Value Per Share = (Total Assets - Total Liabilities) / Shares Outstanding

Price-to-Book Ratio

The P/B ratio compares the stock price to book value per share.

P/B Ratio = Stock Price / Book Value Per Share
  • P/B under 1.0: Stock trades below its net asset value — potentially undervalued
  • P/B 1.0 - 3.0: Reasonable range for most companies
  • P/B above 3.0: Market values the company far above its tangible assets — growth expectations are high

When Book Value Matters Most

Book value is most useful for asset-heavy businesses like banks, insurance companies, and real estate firms where tangible assets are the core of the business.

It's less useful for tech and service companies where value comes from intellectual property, brand, and talent — none of which appear on the balance sheet at full value.

Example:

A bank has book value of $50/share and trades at $45. The P/B is 0.9x — the market values it below its net assets. This could be a bargain if the bank's assets are sound, or a red flag if the market suspects loan losses.

Key Takeaways

  • Book value = assets minus liabilities, per share
  • P/B under 1.0 can signal undervaluation
  • Most useful for banks, insurance, and asset-heavy industries
  • Less meaningful for tech companies with intangible assets
