What Is Intrinsic Value? The Foundation of Smart Investing

Every stock has two prices: what the market charges and what the business is actually worth. Learning the difference is the most important skill in investing.

Two Prices, One Stock

When you look up a stock, you see its market price — what buyers and sellers agreed on today. But beneath that price is something more fundamental: what the business is actually worth based on the cash it generates. That's intrinsic value.

Think of it like buying a house. The listing price might be $500,000, but if the house generates $3,000/month in rental income, you can calculate what it's actually worth to you as an investment. Stocks work the same way.

Why Market Price and Intrinsic Value Diverge

Markets are driven by emotion as much as logic. Fear, greed, news cycles, and herd behavior push prices away from what businesses are truly worth. This creates opportunities.

When the market price drops below intrinsic value, value investors see a buying opportunity. When price soars above intrinsic value, it might be time to sell or avoid the stock entirely.

How Intrinsic Value Is Calculated

The most common approach is a Discounted Earnings Model. The idea is straightforward: a company's value equals all its future earnings, discounted back to today's dollars.

Intrinsic Value = Sum of (Future Earnings / (1 + Discount Rate)^Year) + Terminal Value

The discount rate (typically 10-15%) accounts for the time value of money and investment risk. A dollar earned ten years from now is worth less than a dollar today.

Simple Example:

A company earns $5/share. If earnings grow 8% annually and you use a 12% discount rate, the intrinsic value works out to roughly $96/share. If the stock trades at $70, you have a potential bargain. If it trades at $150, you're overpaying.

The Margin of Safety

Benjamin Graham, the father of value investing, insisted on buying only when the market price is significantly below intrinsic value. This gap is called the margin of safety — your buffer against calculation errors and unexpected events.

Most value investors look for at least a 25% margin of safety before buying. If a stock's intrinsic value is $100, they want to buy at $75 or less.

Key Takeaways

  • Intrinsic value is what a business is actually worth based on its earnings power
  • Market price is what people are willing to pay today — often driven by emotion
  • The gap between the two creates investing opportunities
  • Always demand a margin of safety when buying
