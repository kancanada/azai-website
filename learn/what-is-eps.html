What Is EPS? Understanding Earnings Per Share

Earnings Per Share is the single number that drives most stock valuations. Here is what it means, why it matters, and how to use it.

The Bottom Line, Per Share

Earnings Per Share takes a company's total profit and divides it by the number of shares outstanding. It tells you how much profit the company earned for each share you own.

EPS = Net Income / Shares Outstanding

Example:

A company earns $10 billion in profit and has 1 billion shares outstanding. EPS = $10 billion / 1 billion = $10 per share.

Why EPS Matters

EPS is the foundation of most valuation methods. When analysts calculate intrinsic value, they're projecting future EPS and discounting it back. When you see a P/E ratio, it's price divided by EPS.

Growing EPS is the clearest signal that a business is becoming more valuable. If EPS grows 10% per year consistently, the stock price tends to follow over time.

What to Look For

  • Consistent growth: Companies that grow EPS steadily year over year are the most reliable investments
  • Positive EPS: Negative EPS means the company is losing money — some growth companies do this intentionally, but it adds risk
  • EPS vs revenue growth: EPS growing faster than revenue means the company is becoming more efficient

Basic vs Diluted EPS

Basic EPS uses only existing shares. Diluted EPS includes stock options, convertible bonds, and other instruments that could become shares. Diluted EPS is the more conservative number — always prefer it.

EPS Pitfalls

Companies can artificially boost EPS through stock buybacks (reducing shares outstanding) without actually growing the business. Check that revenue and total earnings are growing alongside EPS.

Key Takeaways

  • EPS = company's profit divided by number of shares
  • It's the foundation of P/E ratios and intrinsic value calculations
  • Look for consistent EPS growth over 5-10 years
  • Use diluted EPS for a more conservative picture
