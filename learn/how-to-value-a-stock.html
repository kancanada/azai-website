How to Value a Stock: A Step-by-Step Guide

Stock valuation is not guessing or gut feeling. Here is a systematic approach anyone can follow to estimate what a stock is truly worth.

Valuation Is a Process, Not a Guess

Many investors buy stocks based on tips, headlines, or gut feelings. Professional value investors use a systematic process. Here's how to value any stock in four steps.

Step 1: Find the Earnings Per Share

Start with trailing EPS — the actual earnings from the past 12 months. This is your anchor, based on real numbers rather than forecasts.

Step 2: Estimate Future Growth

Look at the company's EPS growth rate over the past 5 years. Be conservative — cap your growth estimate at 15-20% even if recent growth was higher. Reversion to the mean is powerful.

Step 3: Apply the Discounted Earnings Model

Project EPS forward 10 years using your growth rate, then discount each year back at 12% (a standard hurdle rate for stocks).

Year N Value = (EPS x (1 + Growth)^N) / (1.12)^N

Add all 10 years plus a terminal value for earnings beyond year 10 (using a lower, sustainable growth rate of 3-4%).

Step 4: Compare to Market Price

If your calculated intrinsic value is $100 and the stock trades at $70, you have a 30% margin of safety. That's a potential buy. If it trades at $130, it's overpriced — move on.

Full Example:

Company XYZ has EPS of $5.00, growing at 10% per year. Using a 12% discount rate over 10 years with 4% terminal growth, intrinsic value works out to approximately $118/share. If XYZ trades at $85, you have a 28% margin of safety.

Key Takeaways

  • Start with real earnings (trailing EPS), not projections
  • Be conservative with growth estimates — it's better to be pleasantly surprised
  • Use a 12% discount rate as your hurdle for stock investments
  • Only buy when market price is well below your calculated value
