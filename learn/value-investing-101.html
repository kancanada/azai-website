Value Investing 101: A Beginner's Complete Guide

Value investing has created more billionaires than any other strategy. Here is how it works and how to start.

Buy Businesses, Not Tickers

Value investing is simple in concept: buy stocks that trade below what the business is actually worth, then wait for the market to recognize the true value. Warren Buffett, Benjamin Graham, and Charlie Munger built enormous wealth with this approach.

The key insight is that stock prices fluctuate far more than business values. A company's revenue doesn't drop 30% because of a bad jobs report, but its stock price might. Value investors exploit this gap.

The Core Principles

1. Calculate intrinsic value

Every investment decision starts with estimating what the business is worth. Use Discounted Earnings Models to project future earnings and discount them to today.

2. Demand a margin of safety

Never pay full price. Always buy at a significant discount to intrinsic value to protect against errors in your analysis.

3. Think long term

Value investing is not trading. Expect to hold positions for years. The market can stay irrational longer than you expect, but eventually, price converges with value.

4. Understand what you own

If you can't explain what the company does and how it makes money in two sentences, you don't know it well enough to invest.

How to Get Started

  1. Learn the key metrics: EPS, P/E ratio, book value
  2. Study a few companies deeply rather than many superficially
  3. Compare the market price to your calculated intrinsic value
  4. Only buy when the margin of safety is sufficient
  5. Be patient — the best opportunities come during market fear

Key Takeaways

  • Buy stocks below their intrinsic value and wait for the market to catch up
  • Always demand a margin of safety — your buffer against being wrong
  • Think in years, not days. Value investing rewards patience.
  • Understand the business before you invest a single dollar
