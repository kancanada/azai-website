ETFs vs Individual Stocks: Which Should You Buy?

Both have a place in your portfolio, but they serve very different purposes. Here is how to decide.

Two Paths to Market Exposure

An ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund) holds dozens or hundreds of stocks in a single package. Instead of buying Apple, you buy a fund that holds Apple plus 499 other companies. Individual stocks give you concentrated ownership in a single business.

When ETFs Win

  • Instant diversification: One purchase gives you exposure to hundreds of companies
  • Lower risk: If one company fails, it barely affects your portfolio
  • Less research required: You don't need to analyze individual companies
  • Low cost: Many ETFs charge under 0.1% in fees annually

When Individual Stocks Win

  • Higher potential returns: Finding an undervalued stock can deliver outsized gains
  • You choose quality: ETFs include both good and bad companies; stock picking lets you own only the best
  • No management fees: You don't pay anyone to manage your portfolio
  • Deeper understanding: You know exactly what you own and why

The Practical Answer

Most investors should do both. Use ETFs as your core portfolio for broad market exposure, then add individual stocks where you have conviction based on your own analysis.

A common split: 70% in broad ETFs (like SPY or VTI), 30% in carefully chosen individual stocks where you've identified a margin of safety.

Key Takeaways

  • ETFs are safer and easier; individual stocks offer higher potential returns
  • Use ETFs for core portfolio, individual stocks for high-conviction ideas
  • Both can be evaluated with valuation principles — ETFs have their own scoring system
