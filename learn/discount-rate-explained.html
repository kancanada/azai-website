What Is a Discount Rate and Why Does It Matter?

The discount rate is the most important — and most misunderstood — variable in stock valuation. Here is what it means in plain English.

Time Is Money — Literally

Would you rather have $100 today or $100 one year from now? Today, obviously. That's because money today can be invested and grow. The discount rate quantifies exactly how much future money is worth less than present money.

How It Works in Valuation

When calculating intrinsic value, you project a company's future earnings and then discount them back to today's value. The discount rate is the percentage you use.

Present Value = Future Earnings / (1 + Discount Rate)^Years

Example:

$100 earned 5 years from now, discounted at 12%, is worth $100 / (1.12)^5 = $56.74 today. The higher the discount rate, the less future earnings are worth.

Why 12%?

Most value investors use 10-15%. The 12% rate is popular because:

  • It roughly equals the long-term stock market average return
  • It accounts for the risk of owning individual stocks
  • It provides a conservative baseline — your investment needs to beat this hurdle

Higher vs Lower Discount Rates

A higher discount rate means you demand more compensation for risk, resulting in a lower intrinsic value (more conservative). A lower rate gives a higher intrinsic value (more aggressive).

Key Takeaways

  • The discount rate converts future earnings into today's dollars
  • 12% is a standard rate for stock valuation — your minimum hurdle
  • Higher rate = more conservative valuation, lower intrinsic value
  • Use a higher rate for riskier companies
